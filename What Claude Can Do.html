01 / The Reframe

Claude isn't software you learn. It's an assistant you brief.

There are no menus to memorize and no features to master. There is one skill: describing what you want the way you'd describe it to a sharp new hire. You give context, you say what done looks like, you review the draft, you say what's off. That's the whole job. Every section below is just proof of how far that one skill goes.

The only mindset shift that matters

Stop asking questions. Start assigning work. "What makes a good welcome email?" gets you a generic lecture. "Write my welcome email" gets you a draft in your voice, because your foundation files are loaded. Same tool. Different results. The difference is you.

02 / Eight Things Claude Can Do

Each of these comes with a real prompt you can paste today. Change the brackets, keep the shape.

1 Write in your voice

Emails, captions, sales pages, video scripts, welcome sequences. Because your foundation files load every session, Claude writes as you, to your person, without being reminded who you are.

Try itWrite an email to my list about [topic]. One idea, one reader, one clear next step. Show me a draft and tell me which line you're least sure sounds like me.
2 Edit like an honest friend

Paste anything you wrote and get real feedback, not compliments. Claude will also check your own drafts against your own rules, which is more useful than it sounds at 11pm before a launch.

Try itHere's a draft I wrote. Check it against my anti-ai-writing-style.md file and my ideal client. Tell me what's working, what's flat, and rewrite only the weak parts: [paste draft]
3 Research the real world

Claude can search the web while it works: what your competitors charge, what real customers complain about in their own words, what's changed in your industry since you last looked.

Try itResearch how real people talk about [my kind of offer] online. Find actual quotes from forums and reviews, what they complain about, what they wish existed, and the exact words they use. Then tell me what my ideal-client file gets right and what it's missing.
4 Organize your files

Claude lives in your folder. It can read everything in it, rename messes, sort downloads into projects, and summarize a folder you haven't opened since March.

Try itLook at everything in my [folder name] folder and tell me what's in there, what's duplicated, and what's clearly outdated. Propose a cleaner structure before moving anything.
5 Build real things

PDF guides, checklists, trackers, calculators, full web pages. If you can describe it, Claude can build it as a file you own. The guide you're reading right now was built exactly this way.

Try itBuild me a one-page checklist my clients can use for [task]. Make it a clean page I can print or send, in my brand voice. Show me the plan before you build it.
6 Do the repetitive stuff

Reformatting fifty product descriptions, turning one long video transcript into ten captions, pulling every testimonial out of a year of screenshots. The boring work that eats your Tuesdays is exactly what Claude is best at.

Try itHere's a transcript of a video I made. Pull out every usable moment and turn them into: 5 short captions, 3 email openers, and 1 longer post. All in my voice: [paste transcript]
7 Think with you

Not everything is a task. Sometimes you need to talk through a pricing decision, a hard email, or whether to kill a project. Claude knows your goals file, so it can push back with your own priorities.

Try itI'm trying to decide [decision]. Read my my-company.md first. Then ask me the questions I'm avoiding, and tell me honestly which option fits what I said my year is about.
8 Remember everything

Every project gets a folder. Every session can end with saved notes. Six weeks from now you can open a project and ask "where were we?" and get a real answer. Your business stops living in your head.

Try itSummarize what we decided and built today. Save it as session-notes.md in this project's folder, and write me an opening line I can paste next time to pick up exactly here.

03 / Skills: Teach It Once, Run It Forever

A skill is a recipe you save once. From then on, one short command runs the whole thing.

Anything you find yourself explaining to Claude twice belongs in a skill. It's a simple file that lives in your folder and tells Claude exactly how you want a job done: the steps, the format, the rules, the things to never do. You don't write it. You describe it, and Claude writes it for you.

Things people like you have turned into skills:

  • A weekly content routine that turns one voice memo into a week of posts
  • A client onboarding flow that builds the welcome doc, the checklist, and the first email in one pass
  • A "does this sound like me?" checker that audits any draft against your own writing rules
  • A Sunday planning session that reads your notes and hands you Monday's priorities
  • An invoice follow-up writer that stays polite on the third reminder when you couldn't
Build your first skillI keep doing this task: [describe it, including how you like it done]. Turn it into a skill I can run with a slash command. Ask me questions until you understand the steps, then create it and tell me what to type to use it.

04 / What Claude Won't Do

Knowing the edges is what makes the middle feel safe.

  • It won't act without you. Claude asks permission before it changes files or runs anything on your computer. You click Yes or No. Nothing happens behind your back.
  • It won't send, post, or spend. Unless you deliberately connect an account and approve an action, Claude drafts and you deliver. The publish button stays yours.
  • It won't always be right. Claude makes mistakes with real confidence. That's why you review drafts, ask "check that" when a number matters, and keep final say on everything with your name on it.
  • It won't read your mind. Vague briefs get vague work, same as with people. The fix is never technical. It's one more sentence of context.
The relationship in one line

Claude proposes. You decide. Every good outcome in this guide runs on that split.

05 / How to Talk to It

Five habits cover 90% of good results:

1

Give the why, not just the what

"Write a caption" is a task. "Write a caption for this reel; the goal is getting moms to comment their answer" is a brief. Briefs win.

2

One task per conversation

Long, wandering threads produce worse work. Finish a thing, start a fresh chat for the next thing.

3

Ask for the plan first

On anything big, add "show me your plan before you build." You'll catch wrong turns in ten seconds instead of ten minutes.

4

Invite questions

End unclear briefs with "ask me anything that's unclear before you start." Claude fills gaps with guesses unless you tell it not to.

5

Say what's off, plainly

"Too salesy, and I'd never say elevate" fixes the draft AND teaches the system, because that note belongs in your writing-rules file. Claude will offer to put it there.

06 / Your First Five Wins

Don't study. Do one of these today. Each takes under fifteen minutes and ends with something real.

Win 1: the email you've been putting offThere's an email I've been avoiding writing. Here's the situation: [explain]. Draft it in my voice, then tell me what you softened and why.
Win 2: this week, plannedHere's everything on my plate this week: [brain dump, messy is fine]. Sort it, tell me what actually matters given my goals file, and give me a realistic plan for the next three days.
Win 3: one piece of content, multipliedHere's a post that did well for me: [paste it]. Turn it into an email, three new captions on the same idea, and two hooks for short videos. My voice, my person.
Win 4: your offer, explained betterRead my my-company.md and ideal-client.md, then explain my offer back to me the way my ideal client would need to hear it. Show me where my current wording is making them work too hard.
Win 5: a folder you're afraid ofMy [Downloads / Desktop / old business folder] is a disaster. Read what's in it, tell me what's there in plain English, and propose what to keep, file, or archive. Don't move anything until I say yes.

07 / Where This Goes

The pattern you'll notice after a month: the work Claude does is only half the value. The other half is that your voice, your audience, your decisions, and your projects now live somewhere outside your head, getting sharper every week. Each session starts smarter than the last one because you corrected it once and never needed to again.

Keep two habits and the whole thing compounds: run /weekly-check-in once a week so your foundation files stay true, and every time you explain something twice, say "make this a skill."
