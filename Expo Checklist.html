Health & Wellness Offline Marketing Lead Generation
Read this first

We're not talking comic-con or bridal fairs. Health and wellness expos are your people. They're already curious. They're already invested in their health. You're not convincing anyone of anything — you're starting a conversation with someone who is already looking for what you have.

There are five stages to a successful expo. Learn these and every event gets easier — and more profitable.

Stage 01 / Registration

Your first step is simple: commit. Everything else flows from here.

1

Find your expo

Search local health and wellness expos in your area. Google "[your city] health and wellness expo 2026" or check local event boards and Facebook events.

2

Lock in your spot early

Popular events fill up fast and late registrations often come with fewer perks. Don't wait until you feel ready. Register first, prepare second.

3

Ask what's included

Before you confirm, ask the organizer:

  • Table and chairs — included, or bring your own?
  • Power outlet access (you'll want this for a TV or laptop)
  • Wi-Fi availability

Know what you're working with before you show up.

4

Put it in your calendar

Date, location, setup time, and event hours. Treat it like a non-negotiable. You're doing this.

Stage 02 / Preparation

This is where most people either shine or spiral. The difference is just being prepared. Cover these four areas before the day of the event.

Part A: Business Supplies

Think of your table as a storefront. It should look intentional before anyone walks in the door. Make it inviting. Make it pop.

Pack List Your table setup
  • Banner or poster with your logo
  • Business cards
  • Flyers
  • QR codes linking to your demo booking, class, or landing page
  • Sample bags of water at 8.5 pH
  • Glass containers for water samples
  • Gallon bags
  • Clean tablecloth
  • Chairs (if not provided by the venue)

Part B: Water Demo Supplies

This is the live science show. This is what hooks people and starts conversations. Let them see the difference with their own eyes. Do not skip this.

Demo Kit Your science show setup
  • Ionfaucet pH stand
  • pH drops
  • Comparison drinks: Sprite, Gatorade, carbonated water, tap water
  • Tomatoes
  • Green tea bags
  • Sesame oil
  • Betadine
  • Vitamin C
  • Kangen 9.5 water
  • Kangen 11.5 water

Part C: Printed Materials

Don't rely on memory. Have the answers ready before someone asks.

Printed Reference materials
  • Medical studies
  • Water usage guides
  • Machine comparison sheets
  • Compensation plans
  • Info binders
If someone asks something you don't know

That is completely fine. Get their number, find the answer, and call them back. That follow-up builds more trust than any scripted answer ever could.

Part D: Giveaways

People love free stuff. This is how you collect leads without feeling salesy. Set up a fishbowl with entry slips. You're not selling — you're inviting.

Giveaways Prize options
  • Free month of water
  • Ukon soap or tea
  • Beauty toner
  • Four weeks of free water demos

Stage 03 / Set Up

Get there early. Give yourself time to set the vibe before anyone walks in. The more interactive and visual your setup, the more people stop — and stopping is the whole game.

1

Arrive at setup time

Not five minutes before the doors open. Early is on time. On time is late. Give yourself enough room to set up without rushing.

2

Start your veggie soak

Use your 11.5 water. Set it up where people can see it working in real time. Real results, happening right in front of them.

3

Pre-set your water samples

Add pH drops before anyone arrives. People should be able to walk up and see something immediately — not wait while you scramble to set up.

4

Loop a video on a TV or laptop

This runs in the background and draws people in while you're already talking to someone else. Two conversations happening at once.

5

Lay your table out deliberately

Make it interactive. Make it visual. You own this space — set it up like you do.

Stage 04 / Execution

You showed up. That alone puts you ahead of most people. Now your job is to connect — not pitch.

The mindset

You are not selling a product. You are offering a solution to something they already care about. Lead with that.

1

Smile and make eye contact

Be the most approachable person in the room. Energy is contagious. If you look bored, people will walk past. If you look like you love what you do, they'll slow down.

2

Ask questions, listen first

Be genuinely curious about the people walking past your table. What brings them here? What are they dealing with? The conversation starts before the pitch ever does.

3

Practice your elevator pitch

Know it well enough that it feels natural, not memorized. If it sounds scripted, start over.

4

Offer a demo to anyone who slows down

The demo is the conversation. Let the water do the talking. You just need to invite them in.

5

Invite people to your next step

A water share, a class, a demo at home. Plant the next touchpoint before they walk away.

Collect their info Get a name and number or have them fill out an entry slip. No contact info means no follow up. No follow up means no sale.
What to avoid

Don't pitch everyone who walks past — connect first. Don't let a slow moment feel like failure. Don't leave your table unattended for long stretches. And don't adjust your integrity because the energy in the room is low — adjust your energy instead.

Stage 05 / Follow Up

Do not skip this step. This is where the sale actually happens.

Most people collect leads at an expo and then do nothing with them. The stack sits on their counter. The contacts go cold. The effort of the day goes to waste. You're not most people.

1

Organize your contacts within 48 hours

Go through every card, entry slip, and contact you collected. Don't let the stack sit. After 48 hours the conversation is already going cold.

2

Send a personal thank-you

Reach out to everyone you had a real conversation with. Thank them for stopping by. Reference something specific from your conversation if you can — it shows you were actually listening.

3

Deliver your freebie wins

If someone entered the drawing, let them know. Fulfill the prize. A giveaway that never gets delivered is worse than no giveaway at all.

4

Invite them to their next step

A demo, a class, a water share. Keep the conversation going. The expo is the introduction — not the close.

5

Keep following up

One message is not follow up. Check in again. Stay in the relationship. Trust builds over time, not in a single conversation at a table.

Stage 07 / Common Mistakes That Cost You

Showing up without a demo

The demo is the conversation starter. Without it, you're just another table people walk past. The science show is the whole point — it gives people something to stop for, something to respond to, something to talk about.

Waiting until the morning of to prepare

Scrambling the day of leads to forgotten items and a stressed-out version of you standing at a half-set table. Pack and check your supplies the night before. If it's not in the bag, it's not coming.

Pitching instead of connecting

Nobody wants to feel like a sales target at a wellness expo. Ask questions first. Be genuinely curious. The pitch comes naturally after the connection — and it lands completely differently when it does.

Collecting leads and disappearing

The follow-up is where the sale happens. If you don't reach out within 48 hours, the conversation goes cold and the day was just an expensive afternoon. Every lead collected without a follow-up is a missed opportunity that you paid for.

Leaving your table unattended

Every minute your table sits empty is a missed conversation. If you have to step away, ask someone to cover you. An empty, unmanned table tells people there's nothing worth stopping for.
