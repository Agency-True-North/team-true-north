02 / Understanding Credit

What Is a Credit Report?

A credit report includes all of your personal information as well as all of the credit card companies and banking institutions that you have transactions with. It is a detailed history of the life of you and your money, showing whether or not you pay your bills on time.

The report can be many pages long since it lists all of your credit accounts: credit cards, mortgages, student loans, auto loans, home equity loans, and more. Each credit reporting agency (there are three in the US, two in Canada) keeps their own separate report — but they contain the same information.

What Is a Credit Score?

A credit score is a three-digit number, usually on a scale of 300 to 850, that estimates your "creditworthiness" — how likely you are to repay borrowed money and pay bills on time.

The highest credit score you can get is 850, although there's not much practical difference between a "perfect" score and an excellent score when it comes to the rates and products you qualify for. Don't stress over hitting 850 — scores fluctuate frequently. Focus on consistent habits.

The Credit Reporting Agencies

The three national bureaus keep credit reports on Americans. In Canada, there are two. They are responsible for providing your information to creditors — the banks are actually responsible for deciding whether or not you qualify for credit.

How Is Your Credit Score Calculated?

35% Payment History — Most Important

The single most important factor. It looks at how many accounts have been paid on time, any collections or negative public records, delinquent accounts, and how recently you made a late payment.

30% Credit Utilization — Very Important

How much of your available credit you're actually using. How much you owe relative to your credit limits. Number of accounts with balances. Aim to keep each card below 30–35% of its limit.

15% Length of Credit History — Somewhat Important

Total length of time tracked by your credit report. Length of time since accounts were opened. The longer you show good credit history, the better your score.

10% Types of Credit — Somewhat Important

Total number of accounts and type of accounts. A mixture of credit types (cards, loans, lines of credit) usually generates better scores than credit cards alone.

10% New Credit — Less Important

Number of accounts recently opened. Number of recent credit inquiries. Time passed since recent inquiries. Opening several new accounts in a short period can lower your score temporarily.

Credit Score Ranges

Exceptional: 800–850

Consistently responsible credit management. More likely to qualify for the lowest interest rates. Long history of on-time payments with low card balances.

Very Good: 740–799

Generally financially responsible. Most payments made on time with fairly low balances relative to credit limits.

Good: 670–739

Often lower due to occasional missed payments or higher utilization. Can still earn a competitive interest rate from most lenders.

Fair: 580–669

Some dings on credit history but no major delinquencies. Still likely to be extended credit, but at higher interest rates. Options are more limited but still available.

03 / Credit Building Techniques

You've made a poor credit decision or two. That doesn't mean it's irreparable.

Improve Your Credit Score — Overnight

1

Get It

Get your credit report. Request it, look it over, and act on what you see. (Refer to the instructions in Section 02.)

2

Fix It

Correct any errors. If there are items you need to dispute, start the dispute immediately. The credit reporting agency has 30 days to resolve the matter. If they are unable to do so within 30 days, they must remove that questionable item from your credit report.

If you can only tackle one credit report at a time, start with the report that shows the middle score — that's the score banks are looking at. Once you've tackled that one, move to the next lowest.

3

Monitor It

Regularly review your credit report. Identity theft is common — a credit monitoring service can watch for unusual items so you don't have to check manually every month.

4

Delete It

Have anything in collections? Call the collection agency and ask if they will delete it from your record if you pay. Instead of showing as "paid," they can delete it entirely. About half of all collection agencies will remove a collection if you pay off the debt — or even a large portion of it. Getting a negative item wiped has a huge and immediate impact on your score.

5

Increase It

For every credit card you have, call and ask them to increase your credit limit. This is usually easier for them to agree to than any other request. You are requesting the increase to boost your credit score — not to spend more. Your score improves when you're not using all of your available credit. The fastest way to accomplish this is to get more available credit, and all you have to do is ask.

6

Spread It

If the credit card company won't increase your limit, spread your debt across all your cards. The FICO system wants you to use only 30–35% of your available credit on each card. If a card has a $1,000 limit, aim to only carry $300 on it.

7

Pay It

To get your cards down to that 30–35%, pay down balances wherever possible. Get each card to the magic 30% level. If you can pay off cards entirely, do it.

8

Find It

Have savings sitting in the bank? If you have a debt in collections or a high balance, it's time to use it. Unless you're earning significant interest on savings, you're better off paying off problem areas. If you don't have savings, consider borrowing from family or friends at a lower rate than a bank would charge — or no interest at all.

9

Check It

After you use these methods, verify that your credit report actually reflects your work. If you paid off a collection, paid down a balance, or disputed an item — make sure it shows. If fixes don't appear, contact the credit bureaus and send proof of payment.

Statute of Limitations on Debt

If you had a debt in the past that you couldn't pay, the creditor may have written it off as uncollectible and collection agencies have been after you ever since. Here's what most people don't know:

Paying a debt that is too old is not required

What makes a debt "too old" depends on your state or province's statute of limitations — an expiration date on collectible debt. If a debt was written off six years ago and there has been no activity on your credit report since, you are likely free of that debt forever.

You have more power than they want you to think

A creditor may reach out near the end of the statute period using fear tactics — threats of lawsuits, urgent language. Don't fall for it. When the collector calls, simply tell them the debt is no longer collectible — then hang up. Never admit to owning a debt or the statute could restart. Send a written letter telling them to stop calling you. A sample letter is included below.

Cut Your Rates

Call your credit card companies (the number is on the back of your card) and ask what your current interest rate is — then ask them to lower it. If the first person you speak to won't help, ask for a supervisor. If they say no, call back. The vast majority of the time, they will lower your rate simply because you asked. You can also mention you have a competitive offer from another bank and will transfer your balance unless they lower your rate.

Use 0% Balance Transfer Promotions

If you have a credit card with available balance, call your credit card company and ask if they have a 0% balance transfer promotion. A balance transfer moves debt from one account to another — and at 0% interest, you pay nothing on that transferred balance for the promotional period. This significantly lowers the total interest you're currently paying.

More Strategies: Bad Credit to Good Credit

1

Pay on time

Every late payment is a negative factor on your credit report. Non-negotiable.

2

Pay more

Make at least the minimum payment every month. If at all possible, pay more than the minimum. The minimum is calculated as a flat percentage of your balance, or a percentage plus interest and fees.

3

Don't skip

Keep paying. If an account goes to collections, it can harm your score for up to seven years. If you're struggling to make payments, call the creditor and work out a different arrangement. Pay whatever you can and work responsibly with them.

4

Don't close old accounts

If you have a good track record with an old account, keep it open — even if you don't use it. One of the factors in your credit score is how long you've had credit with each creditor. Closing accounts shortens your history.

5

Stay below 30–35%

Lenders don't want to see you maxed out. Your score suffers if you carry a balance over 34% of the available credit limit on any account.

6

Don't open accounts impulsively

Every new account lowers your score slightly. Don't open an account for a free gift or a discount offer. Be strategic.

7

Avoid excessive inquiries

Every credit application triggers an inquiry, which shows on your report for two years. Don't apply for multiple loans and credit cards in a short period. If you open a new account, wait several months before applying again.

Building Credit from Scratch

If you have no credit history, here's how to start:

  • Apply for a secured credit card. Requires a deposit that serves as your credit limit — low risk, builds history fast. Good options: Discover Secured, Capital One Secured.
  • Become an authorized user on someone else's card. Builds credit without requiring you to make payments.
  • Apply for a credit-builder loan. Designed specifically to establish credit through regular, reported payments. Self (formerly Self Lender) is a well-known option — you make monthly payments into a savings account and the payments are reported to all three bureaus.
  • Chime Credit Builder Card — no annual fee, no minimum security deposit.
  • Bright Money app — helps build credit and manage payments automatically.
Newer tool: Experian Boost (USA)

Experian Boost is a free service that lets you add on-time utility, phone, and streaming subscription payments (Netflix, Spotify, etc.) directly to your Experian credit file. It can raise your score immediately and costs nothing. Add Experian Boost here.

A timing trick most people miss

Your credit utilization is calculated based on the balance reported on your statement closing date — not your payment due date. If you pay down your balance before the statement closes (not just before the due date), your reported utilization will be lower, which means a better score. Even if you pay in full every month, the timing matters.

Sample Letter to a Debt Collector

[Date]

[Your Name]
[Your Address]


[Collector's Name]
[Collector's Address]


Dear [name of collector or creditor],


I'm writing in response to your [phone call / letter] of [date]. To the best of my knowledge, I do not owe that debt.

I have not been contacted by anyone about this alleged debt, and I ask that you send me any and all information you have on file: the amount, the creditor, and proof that you are licensed to collect debts in my state or province.

I am disputing this alleged debt. If you are not the party who owns this debt, please forward my letter to the creditor who does so that they are notified of this dispute.


Sincerely,
[Your Name]


Send via certified mail with return receipt requested. Keep a copy for your records. Keep detailed records of all communications — phone and written — with debt collectors.
