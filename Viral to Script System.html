Read this before you script anything

Viral content is research, not source material. The video that stopped your thumb earned its views through mechanics — the hook shape, the structure, the way it held attention to the last second. Those mechanics transfer. The topic does not. The moment you borrow the topic, you inherit its audience — and its audience is not your buyer.

This guide is the whole system: collect, extract the mechanic, screen it, re-anchor it on the woman we serve, script it in one of two formats, log it in the tracker. Up to ten scripts per batch.

01 / Find the Virals — SortFeed Setup

SortFeed is a free Chrome extension that sorts any Instagram profile, hashtag, or saved collection by what actually performed. It's how the batch gets built. Set it up once, then it's a two-minute step in the weekly rhythm.

One-Time Setup

  • Go to sortfeed.com and click the button at the top right — "Add to Chrome"
  • Install it from the Chrome Web Store — the listing is "Sort Feed — works on Instagram, TikTok & Facebook"
  • Open Instagram in Chrome like normal — that's it, the extension rides along

Every Run

  1. Go to a creator's profile in your niche — or search a hashtag, or open a collection you've saved.
  2. Click the SortFeed icon in your browser toolbar. A control panel opens right on the page.
  3. Sort by Views — or by Outlier Score, which surfaces the reels that beat that creator's own average. Outlier is often the more useful sort.
  4. Set the range — a number of posts or a date window — so it loads enough of the feed to sort deep.
  5. Export the sorted list to Google Sheets or CSV, or transcribe the top reels so you can read the hooks.
What comes out of this step

The transcript is the handoff. For each reel that makes the batch: the hook, the format, the view count, and the source page. That's the raw material everything below runs on.

02 / Work Your Niche

There's no global search in SortFeed — you build the niche yourself. That's not a limitation. The list you build becomes an asset you run every single week.

  1. Make your niche map. A list of 10–20 creators who are crushing it in your space, plus 5–10 hashtags. Keep it in one doc.
  2. Run SortFeed on each one. Sort by views or outlier score, and skim the top 5–10 reels per creator.
  3. Let the giants surface themselves. The 10M-view reels rise to the top on their own. You don't filter for 10M — you sort descending and eyeball the cutoff. A hard "only show me 10M+" threshold isn't a feature.
Outlier score beats raw views

A reel that did 2M on an account that normally gets 40K taught the algorithm something repeatable — that's a mechanic worth extracting. A 10M reel on a famous account is often just fame being famous, and fame doesn't transfer. When in doubt, sort by outlier.

03 / How the System Works

Once a week, collect a batch of viral videos — the SortFeed run from sections 01 and 02, plus anything in your saved folder. For each one, capture four things: the hook, the format, the view count, and the source page. That batch goes in. Shoot-ready scripts come out.

The flow is always the same, one video at a time:

  1. Extract the mechanic — what made it work, in one line. Never the topic.
  2. Screen it — would this mechanic drag in the wrong audience? If yes, skip the video.
  3. Re-anchor it — rebuild the mechanic on our world and our woman.
  4. Script it — voiceover or talking head, tagged, with everything needed to film.
  5. Log it — one row in the tracker, so you learn which mechanics work on your audience.
The creative is the targeting

Instagram and Meta show your content to people who resemble the people it sounds like. Script like a money-channel and the algorithm will hand you the money-crowd — viewers who will never buy, and who crowd out the woman who would. The words in your script decide who sees your reel. Treat every line as targeting.

04 / The One Rule That Outranks Everything

1
The Rule
Model the format and the hook mechanics. Never the topic.

The mechanic is portable — an open loop, a countdown structure, a payoff held to the last line, a contrast hook. The topic is not portable. A topic arrives carrying its own audience, its own vocabulary, and its own expectations. Take the skeleton. Leave the skin.

✕
Don't

Rewrite a "$400 a day side hustle" viral with your own spin on the numbers.

✓
Do

Take its open-loop hook shape and aim it at her Tuesday — the morning that doesn't belong to her.

✕
Don't

Copy a screenshot-reveal video and swap in your own dashboard.

✓
Do

Copy the reveal timing — hold the payoff to the final beat so she watches to the end.

✕
Don't

Borrow a "day in the life of a 22-year-old founder" premise.

✓
Do

Borrow the time-stamped structure and fill it with a morning that runs on a system instead of a person.

Naming the mechanic is the skill. Practice writing it in one line: "question hook + numbered list + payoff at the end" — "silent b-roll + one text line that lands twice" — "contrast open: what they think vs. what it is." If you can't name the mechanic without describing the topic, you haven't found the mechanic yet.

05 / The Kill List

Some language attracts an audience that will never buy — and pushes away the one who would. No view count justifies it. If modeling a viral pulls any of this into your script, cut the line or skip the video.

  • Money-hype dialect — "make money online," "side hustle," "$X a day," "passive income" as a hype phrase, screenshots-of-earnings energy
  • Marketer vocabulary — affiliate links, funnels, agencies, day-trading, crypto, "scale to six figures"
  • Hustle-bro framing — grind worship, 4 a.m. flexing, "while everyone else sleeps"
  • Bait mechanics — "comment WORD below," "DM me for info," follow-for-part-two, emoji bait
  • Income numbers of any kind — no earnings claims, no "typical results," ever, in public content
When the mechanic can't leave its topic behind

Some virals only work because of what they're about — the mechanic and the topic are the same thing. Skip those and note why. A skipped viral costs you nothing. The wrong audience costs you the right one.

06 / Re-Anchor on Her

Every script is rebuilt for one person: a woman in her 30s to 50s with the markers of success and none of the ease. She's on the couch after the kids are down, scrolling, tired, still hoping. She is who the mechanic gets aimed at — no one else.

Anchor every script in our world:

  • The choice. She doesn't have to do anything. She gets to choose everything. That certainty is the spine.
  • Health, money, relationships, freedom. The four proofs. Concrete, sensory, hers — never vague aspiration.
  • Systems over grinding. The life runs on a system, not on her sprinting.
  • Build a Bigger Table. Everyone has a seat. Nothing we publish should make her feel she has to earn hers first.
Lead from certainty

Never name the doubt — not to deny it, not to joke about it, not to defang it. Naming it plants it. Stand in the positive opposite: choice, proof, systems. Say what is true and let it be enough.

07 / The Kallaway 8 Pass

Once the script is re-anchored, run it through these eight principles. They're layered on top of the voice — they never override it.

  1. Curiosity & contrast — pop a question in her head, answer it, pop the next one. The strongest hooks put expectation and reality side by side.
  2. Speed to value — three seconds to earn the next ten. Cut every line of throat-clearing.
  3. Value density — every line pays or it goes.
  4. Clarity — one idea per beat, active voice. Say the key point twice: once precise, once stupid-simple with a picture.
  5. Absorption — make it easy for her to see herself inside it. Shrink the distance between watching and recognizing.
  6. Anticipation — tease the payoff early, deliver it at the end. That's what holds her past second seven.
  7. Emotional resonance — pick the one emotion the script transfers, then choose every word for it.
  8. Rhythm — mostly short beats, varied length. Read it out loud before you film it. If you stumble, she scrolls.

08 / Two Formats — VO and TH

Every script gets tagged one of two ways before it's finished. The split is roughly 70/30 — voiceover is the workhorse, talking head is the weekly exception.

VO Voiceover Over Life Footage — the workhorse

A scripted voiceover laid over 20–30 seconds of real footage from your actual day — the coffee pour, the drive, the desk, the kitchen, the porch. Shot in passing, on your phone, no setup. If a shot list requires lighting, a hair decision, or a scheduled shoot, it's written wrong.

A VO script includes: the hook line, the voiceover beats, the caption, and a b-roll shot list — one shot per beat, all capturable during a normal day.

TH Talking Head — one per week, at most

You, to camera. Reserved for messages that lose weight without a face on them — contrarian beliefs, hard truths, the named frameworks. If the message survives as a voiceover, it's a VO.

A TH script includes: the spoken hook, the body beats, on-screen text cues, and the caption.

09 / What Every Script Includes

  • Format tag — VO or TH, decided before scripting, not after
  • Modeled from — the source page and the mechanic, each in one line
  • Hook, first three seconds — the spoken version and the on-screen version, written separately
  • Beat-by-beat body — numbered, one idea per beat
  • On-screen text cues — which beat gets which text
  • B-roll shot list — VO only; one real-life shot per beat
  • Caption — short, second person, opens a door
  • CTA — or a deliberate decision to run without one
  • Tracker row — pre-filled, ready to paste

10 / Sample Script (VO)

One finished script, front to back. Mechanic modeled: a hold-the-payoff structure from a viral with 2.1M views — the last line is the reason to stay. Topic left behind.

Hook  —  0–3 Seconds

No one warned you that the hardest part of success is that it needs you every single day.

On-screen: "the part no one says out loud" — b-roll: coffee pouring, slow.
Beats

1. You built the thing. The income, the title, the calendar that fills itself.

2. And somewhere in there, the mornings stopped belonging to you.

3. The women who got them back didn't work harder. They changed what the work runs on — a system that doesn't need them standing in the middle of it.

4. Not busy. Free.

B-roll: 1 — laptop open on a full inbox. 2 — car door closing in the dark. 3 — porch, sunrise, both hands on the mug. 4 — laptop closing.
Caption

The morning is the tell. If it starts with everyone else's needs, the system is running you. There's a version where the coffee is still hot and the morning is still yours.

CTA

None on this one. Recognition is the job — the door stays visible in the bio.

11 / CTA Rules

A door opens. It never closes on her. Most scripts carry no hard CTA at all — if she sees herself in it, feels less alone, and feels a door open, the reel did its work.

  • When a CTA earns its place, it points to the True North Masterclass — thirty minutes, linked in bio, when she's ready. Calm, specific, zero urgency.
  • Never "DM me." Never a countdown. Never a pitch. Anything that sounds like a funnel gets cut.
  • Naming matters: the True North Masterclass is the class. The True North Method is the app. Don't swap them.
The test

Read the last line and ask: does it open a door or reach for her wallet? If it reaches, rewrite it or delete it. She can feel the difference from across the room — and so can the algorithm.

12 / The Voice Check

Run every finished script through this list before it's marked ready. One miss sends it back.

  • Second person, all the way through. The picture lives in her eyes, not yours.
  • No exclamation marks. No all-caps lines. No emoji bait.
  • None of the words that break the voice — manifest, transform, unlock, empower, journey, abundance, boss babe, side hustle, "this opportunity."
  • No guilt as a motivator — especially around motherhood, health, or success.
  • No income numbers. That conversation lives inside the masterclass, never in a reel.
  • The doubt is never named. The script stands in certainty and choice.
  • Read out loud once. If it sounds like a funnel, kill it and start from the mechanic again.

13 / The Content Tracker

One running table, one row per script, kept forever. This is how you find out which mechanics work on your audience — not the viral's audience. Keep it in one place: a markdown file, a doc, or a sheet. Never one tracker per batch.

Status moves in one direction: scripted → shot → posted. When a reel goes up, fill the posted date. A week later, log the performance — views, saves, follows, and whether the right woman showed up in the comments. That last column is the whole reason the tracker exists.

14 / Weekly Batch Checklist

  • Collect up to 10 virals — hook, format, view count, source page for each
  • Write the mechanic for each in one line, topic-free
  • Run the kill list — skip anything that can't leave its topic behind
  • Re-anchor each survivor on her — choice, the four proofs, systems, the bigger table
  • Draft — VO by default, at most one TH in the batch
  • Kallaway 8 pass on every draft
  • Voice check on every draft — one miss sends it back
  • Add tracker rows, status "scripted"
  • Pull the b-roll shot lists into one film list for the week
The standard

Ten scripts that sound like you beat a hundred that sound like the internet. The viral gave you the skeleton. The voice, the woman, and the table are ours. Protect all three.
