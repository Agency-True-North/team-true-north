01 / What This Series Does

This is the long game. Some women need months of relationship before they're ready to move. This series keeps you in their inbox — consistently, with value — until that moment comes.

The leads entering Series 6 have been through your entire funnel. They registered, watched (or didn't watch) the webclass, received the cart close emails, went through seven days of trust-building, and got another shot at the webclass in the Cart Reopen. They're still here. They haven't unsubscribed. That matters.

This series isn't a last-ditch push. It's a relationship. You show up consistently with value — things that genuinely help, entertain, or challenge her — and you weave in soft invitations back to the masterclass when it makes sense. Over time, that consistency does the work.

At a glance Series 6 overview
  • Trigger: End of Series 5 (Cart Reopen) — added via "Add to Workflow" in Series 5
  • Duration: Ongoing — no end date, no final email
  • Structure: Value emails + push emails, oscillating
  • Links to: Evergreen Webclass (on push emails only)
  • Cadence: Your choice — see Section 02
  • Built from: Scratch inside GHL — see Section 06 for full setup

02 / Your Cadence

There's no required schedule. The only rule is consistency. An email every other day you actually send beats a daily email you burn out on in three weeks.

Monday, Wednesday, Friday is one option — but it's not the only one. Some affiliates do Tuesday and Thursday. Some do every day once they're in a rhythm. Some send three times a week and ramp up over time. What matters is that the leads in this list hear from you on a predictable schedule, and that you can sustain it.

Cadence options How to think about frequency
  • Starting out: 2–3 times per week. Sustainable, builds the habit, gives you time to write quality content.
  • Once you're in rhythm: Ramp toward daily if you can. Daily email is the goal — presence builds trust faster than occasional contact.
  • The pattern: 2–3 value emails, then 1 push email, then repeat. Adjust the ratio based on what's resonating with your audience.
  • Timing: Pick a consistent send time and stick to it. Mid-morning (8–10AM) typically performs well, but any time you can commit to works.
Build the sequence as you go

You don't need to write 60 emails before you start. Build out two to three weeks of content, set it live, and keep writing. When your sequence runs out of scheduled emails, the lead just stays on the list — GHL won't remove them. You can always add more email actions to the workflow.

03 / Content Ideas

Every email is either a value email or a push email. Value keeps her warm and builds the relationship. Pushes bring her back to the masterclass. Both types serve the same person — they just serve her differently.

Value email ideas

Podcasts Share what you're listening to

Pick an episode that genuinely moved you or made you think. Tell them why. Connect it back to something your ICP is navigating — exhaustion, money, identity, time. You don't have to tie it to the business at all. Just share something real.

  • An episode about burnout, rest, or redefining productivity
  • An interview with a woman who rebuilt her life on her own terms
  • Something that challenged how you think about money or worth
  • A story of someone who started late, started over, or started small
Books Share what you're reading

It doesn't have to be a business book. It can be a memoir, a novel, something that just hit differently this week. Share a line that stayed with you. Tell them what it made you think about. One paragraph is enough.

  • Books about women navigating identity shifts
  • Books about wealth, financial independence, or breaking generational patterns
  • Books about rest, boundary-setting, or the cost of overgiving
  • A line from something you reread that landed differently this time
Stories Tell something real from your week

A moment. An observation. Something that happened and made you think of her. Stories don't have to be polished or profound. They just have to be honest.

  • A conversation with your kid that made you think about why you're building this
  • A moment where the old version of you would have reacted differently
  • Something a woman on your team said that stopped you
  • A real win — small or large — that reminded you why this works
  • A moment of doubt, and what you did with it
Reframes Shift how she sees something

These are short emails that offer a different angle on something she's probably thinking. No pitch. Just a new way of looking at it.

  • A different way to think about "not having time"
  • What "starting over" actually looks like in practice vs. what people imagine
  • The difference between busy and productive — and what your life looked like when you confused them
  • What she's actually choosing when she keeps waiting for the right moment

Push email ideas

Push emails Bringing her back to the masterclass

Every few value emails, send a soft invitation back to the masterclass. The best push emails connect directly to something you covered recently in a value email — they don't feel like a gear shift, they feel like a natural next step.

  • Tie back to the last value email: "Last week I shared [podcast/book/moment]. The masterclass goes deeper on exactly this — specifically the part about [topic]."
  • Straight, honest ask: "I've been showing up in your inbox for a while now. If you're still here, you're still curious. The masterclass is the next step — it's there when you're ready."
  • Story-led push: Tell a short story about a woman who waited, then finally watched, then moved. Keep it real. Don't oversell what happened after.
  • The check-in: "I want to ask you something directly. What's actually standing between you and watching this? Because whatever it is, I've probably felt it too."

04 / The Psychology of the Long Game

Every value email is also addressing an objection — she just doesn't know it. That's what makes this series work. You're not overcoming resistance. You're dissolving it over time, through relationship.

The leads in this series have hesitations. Some of them know what those hesitations are. Most don't. Below are the objections that are most likely living beneath the surface for your ICP, and the kind of email that addresses each one without ever naming it directly.

The real objection What it sounds like The email type that addresses it
Time "I don't have time to add one more thing." Podcast or reframe email about how this fits into life as it already is — not in addition to it. Stories that show what it looked like to start small.
Skepticism "I've tried things before and been let down." Tell your own story of doubt. Name the specific moment you almost didn't start. Don't resolve it too neatly — honesty about the uncertainty is more convincing than a tidy ending.
Identity "I'm not the kind of person who does things like this." Stories about women who looked nothing like what they imagined when they started. Reframes about what "this kind of person" actually looks like in real life.
Fear of hoping "What if I get excited and it still doesn't work?" Honest stories about the cost of staying where you are versus the cost of trying. Don't promise outcomes — describe what the decision itself felt like, and what happened when women made it anyway.
Not the right time "Maybe after [season/event/situation changes]." Future pacing emails — describe what life looks like six months or a year into building this. Not a fantasy. A realistic, specific version of what changes and what's still hard. Then describe what life looks like in six months of not starting.
Loneliness / no support "I don't have anyone in my life who would get this." Community emails — glimpses into what being on the team actually feels like. A conversation with someone on your team. What it felt like the first time you were in a room full of women who wanted the same things.
"I don't know what I'm doing" "I have no business background, I'm not good at sales." Normalize not knowing. Tell a story about a specific skill you didn't have when you started. Don't promise a learning curve that doesn't exist — show what it actually looks like to build as you go.
You don't have to plan this precisely

You don't need to map out which objection you're addressing in each email before you write it. If you're writing from a real place — sharing things you genuinely care about, telling stories that actually happened — you will naturally address these things. This table is here to give you language for what's underneath when you're stuck, not to turn your emails into a strategy exercise.

05 / An Example Email

One example to show the format and tone. Value emails are short, personal, and have no agenda beyond connection.

Example: Podcast recommendation
Subject This episode stopped me mid-walk.
Preview I've been thinking about what she said ever since.

Hey,

I was on a walk last week listening to Podcast Name, and there was a moment where I had to stop and just stand there for a second.

The host asked her guest: "What would you have done differently if you'd known earlier that the system wasn't built for you?"

And the guest said, "I would have stopped trying to be better at the system and started building something outside of it a lot sooner."

I've been thinking about that ever since.

Because I think so many of us spend years getting really good at something that was never going to give us what we actually needed. And we call that ambition. But sometimes it's just loyalty to a system that isn't loyal back.

Anyway. I thought you might want to listen. The episode is called Episode Name. Worth it.

With love,
Your Name

What makes this work

No ask. No link. No CTA. Just a real thing that landed for you, shared with someone you're thinking about. The next email might be another value email. Or it might be a push email that says: "The masterclass talks about exactly this — building outside a system that isn't working. If what I shared last week resonated, this is the next step." That's the oscillation. Value earns the push. The push lands because the value was real.

06 / Building It in GHL

Unlike the previous series, this workflow doesn't exist yet. You're building it from scratch. Follow these steps in order.

Write your emails first

Before you open GHL, write two to three weeks of emails in Google Docs. Have them ready to paste in. Building the workflow is fast — writing the emails takes longer. Don't try to do both at once inside GHL.

Step 1 Go to the correct workflow folder
  • Go to Automations > Workflows in GHL.
  • Open: 2. Pipeline 1 & 2 (The True North Method)
  • Click Create Workflow and name it: 6- Long Term Nurture
Step 2 Add the tag action

This tags each lead when they enter the sequence, so you can identify and filter them later.

  • Click the + to add a new action.
  • Search for and select Add Tag.
  • Type long term nurture in the tag field. GHL will create the tag automatically.
  • Click Save Action.
Step 3 Add a Wait step

The Wait step controls when each email goes out. Use "Until a recurring window opens" to set the days and times you want to send.

  • Click + to add another action.
  • Search for and select Wait.
  • Choose Until a recurring window opens.
  • Select the days and times for your first send. This is where you set your cadence.
  • Click Save Action.
Step 4 Add your first email
  • Click + to add another action.
  • Search for and select Email.
  • Paste in your subject line, preview text, and email body.
  • Click Save Action.
Step 5 Repeat for each email in your sequence

After each email action, add another Wait, then another Email. Repeat this pattern for every email in your sequence. Save Action after every single step.

  • Click Save in the top right corner when your full sequence is added.
  • Click Publish to make the workflow active.
Step 6 Connect Series 5 to Series 6

Leads won't enter this workflow automatically — you need to tell Series 5 to hand them off. Here's how:

  • Go back to the Series 5 (Cart Reopen) workflow.
  • Scroll to the very bottom of the workflow — after the last email action.
  • Click + and search for Add to Workflow.
  • Select 6- Long Term Nurture from the list.
  • Click Save Action, then click Save in the top right and Publish.
Keep adding to the sequence over time

When you run out of scheduled emails, leads just sit on the list — they don't go anywhere or get removed. Go back into the workflow any time and add more Wait + Email pairs to extend the sequence. You can also duplicate the workflow and build a second version if your first one gets long.
